# East Crazies EA Soils Specialist Report Scope Of Work

Date: 2026-05-07

This scope of work defines the fieldwork, analysis, and deliverables needed to prepare a Soils
Specialist Report for the East Crazy Inspiration Divide Land Exchange Environmental Assessment on the
Custer Gallatin National Forest. It is written so a consultant, Forest Service soils specialist, or
Custer Gallatin interdisciplinary resource specialist can understand what is necessary to complete the
soils report and provide report-ready input to the EA.

This SOW is a Project SOW planning and contracting support artifact. It is not a final SOW award
document, an applicability decision, a compliance finding, legal advice, a legal sufficiency
determination, or a final agency decision.

## Source Basis

This SOW was reorganized from current source documents and East Crazies package documents.

Source requirements:

- 36 CFR 220.4 and 36 CFR 220.7 require the EA record to support planning, decision-making, and
  public disclosure and to include need, proposed action, alternatives, environmental effects, and
  agencies/persons consulted.
- Region 1 FSM 2550 Soil Management Supplement requires management practices that maintain or
  improve soil quality. It identifies detrimental soil disturbance (DSD) as compaction, rutting,
  displacement, severe burning, surface erosion, loss of surface organic matter, and soil mass
  movement; requires new activities to keep cumulative detrimental soil conditions within the
  applicable activity-area standard; and recommends photo points, visual methods, transects, and
  detailed sampling when visual methods are not enough.
- The Custer Gallatin Land Management Plan soil direction includes FW-DC-SOIL-01 for inherent soil
  productivity, FW-DC-SOIL-02 and FW-DC-SOIL-03 for organic substrates and coarse woody debris,
  FW-STD-SOIL-01 for detrimental soil conditions, and FW-GDL-SOIL-01 through FW-GDL-SOIL-08 for
  equipment slope limits, blading depth, restoration methods, scarification depth, landslide
  avoidance, soil pit/trench handling, coarse woody debris, and burn pile restoration.
- Forest Service Soil Quality Monitoring materials, including the Forest Soil Disturbance Monitoring
  Protocol and Soil-Disturbance Field Guide, supply the expected soil-disturbance class framework.
- NRCS Web Soil Survey and SSURGO supply the official soil map units, soil properties,
  interpretations, and GIS data needed for baseline soil conditions.
- National Core BMPs and applicable Montana, Forest Service, and Custer Gallatin BMP direction
  supply implementation controls for erosion, sediment, drainage, water quality, and invasive
  species.

Specialist-report model reviewed:

- `Soils Report Revised_Anderson 2023.pdf`, South Plateau Landscape Area Treatment Project. That
  report is the local specialist-report model for structure and level of detail. It includes
  affected environment, applicable Forest Plan standards and guidelines, methodology, spatial and
  temporal scale, resource indicators, DSD analysis, CWD analysis where vegetation management is
  involved, direct and indirect effects, cumulative effects, Forest Plan compliance, references, and
  quantified tables for DSD and mitigation assumptions.

What the local soils report model did:

- identified the affected environment and existing condition before effects analysis;
- stated the applicable Forest Plan standards and guidelines, including DSD and CWD direction;
- defined spatial and temporal scales before comparing alternatives;
- selected resource indicators and indicator measures;
- used field and remote sampling to identify existing DSD;
- calculated proposed and cumulative DSD where an activity area could be defined;
- prescribed mitigation or dropped units where calculated DSD would exceed the applicable
  threshold;
- analyzed direct, indirect, and cumulative effects by alternative;
- closed with Forest Plan compliance, references, and supporting tables.

This SOW carries that report structure forward, but it does not assume East Crazies requires the same
quantified DSD treatment. East Crazies is a land exchange with limited ground disturbance, dominated
by trail, trailhead, parcel-restriction, wetland/riparian, access, and sediment-connection questions.
The soils specialist must therefore either confirm the existing East Crazies no-meaningful-soil-effect
rationale or revise it with field evidence, GIS evidence, and a clear Forest Plan rationale.

East Crazies documents reviewed:

- East Crazy Inspiration Divide Final Environmental Assessment, January 2025.
- East Crazies Plan Consistency Table.
- East Crazies Roads, Trails, and Access Report.
- East Crazies Wetlands Report.
- Post Exchange Federal Parcels and Restrictions.
- East Crazies Project SOW intake fixture and generated Project SOW package smoke output.

East Crazies soils-analysis reality:

- The East Crazies final specialist report folder does not include a standalone soils specialist
  report.
- The Plan Consistency Table addressed soils directly. It found the only ground-disturbing
  activities were the new Sweet Trunk Trail and the Inspiration Divide Trail reroute; stated those
  activities would not affect soil resources or organic substrates at a large enough scale to be
  meaningful; treated FW-STD-SOIL-01 as not applicable because trail building was not one of the
  activities to which that standard was intended to apply and defining the activity area for a linear
  trail was problematic; and marked most soil guidelines not applicable because the project did not
  include timber harvest, temporary road construction, blading, vegetation management, reforestation,
  soil pits/trenches, pile burning, or ground equipment on steep grades.
- The Plan Consistency Table did apply related road/trail and watershed guidelines to trail
  construction: hydrologically disconnect trail drainage from riparian management zones and water
  bodies, avoid high mass-wasting potential if found during trail construction, and harden trail or
  stream crossings to protect streambeds, banks, and approaches.
- The final EA described Sweet Trunk Trail No. 274 as a new 22-mile trail, the Inspiration Divide
  Trail No. 8 relocation as about 1,500 feet, and Big Timber Canyon Trailhead improvements as
  potentially including resurfacing, additional parking, toilet facilities, and an interpretive kiosk.
  The EA also stated trail construction could deliver sediment where the trail nears or crosses
  waterbodies, but that magnitude and duration would be relatively low with design features and LMP
  protections; the Trail No. 8 reroute would remove an unnecessary crossing of an unnamed
  intermittent stream and adjacent wetland.
- The Wetlands Report documented actual fieldwork on September 23-28, 2022. A Custer Gallatin
  wetland scientist assessed Federal Parcels 1 and 2 in a meandering manner; excavated soil pits;
  evaluated hydric soil indicators, vegetation, and hydrology; mapped wetland boundaries with
  resource-grade GPS; used confirmation test pits where full sample points were not collected; and
  used those field results to calibrate a refined preliminary wetland assessment across the remaining
  parcels using MTNHP, Google Earth, ESRI imagery, NHD, NWI, professional judgment, and GIS.
- Post-exchange restrictions include no-subdivision/no-mineral-development deed restrictions on
  Federal Parcels 1-4, a cultural protective covenant on Parcel 6, and wetland protective covenants
  on Parcels 7-10.

## Fieldwork

Fieldwork must verify the soils facts needed to support the Soils Specialist Report. It must be scoped
to East Crazies conditions and to the soil-resource requirements above.

### Fieldwork Objectives

The field effort must answer these questions:

- Where would ground disturbance occur from the Sweet Trunk Trail, Inspiration Divide Trail reroute,
  Big Timber Canyon Trailhead improvements, easements, wetland/riparian protections, grazing-related
  improvements, or parcel-management restrictions?
- Are there soil map units, landforms, slopes, hydric soils, unstable areas, compacted areas, eroding
  areas, or sediment-routing pathways that require design criteria, mitigation, or monitoring?
- Do soil conditions support the Plan Consistency Table conclusion that the project would not affect
  soil resources or organic substrates at a meaningful scale?
- If FW-STD-SOIL-01 is not applied to the linear trail features, what soil-quality indicators and
  activity-area rationale explain that decision?
- What BMPs, drainage controls, revegetation requirements, and post-construction monitoring are
  necessary for trail, trailhead, wetland/riparian, and access-related work?

### Pre-Field Data Review

Before field mobilization, compile and review:

- proposed action, alternatives, final EA, Plan Consistency Table, Roads/Trails/Access Report,
  Wetlands Report, maps, and decision documents;
- Sweet Trunk Trail No. 274 alignment, Inspiration Divide Trail No. 8 reroute, Big Timber Canyon
  Trailhead improvement footprint, road/trail easements, stream crossings, wetland/riparian areas,
  and parcel boundaries;
- federal disposal parcels and non-federal acquisition parcels, including post-exchange restrictions
  and any restrictions that affect soil, wetland, mineral, or development conditions;
- NRCS Web Soil Survey/SSURGO soil map units, hydrologic soil groups, erosion hazard, slope,
  drainage, compaction/rutting limitations, revegetation limitations, and hydric soil ratings;
- Custer Gallatin soil, watershed, road/trail, riparian, recreation, invasive species, and
  recommended-wilderness/backcountry plan components that could affect soil work;
- NHD, NWI, MTNHP wetland/riparian data, DEM/slope data, aerial imagery, existing route layers,
  grazing allotment and improvement layers, and any available landslide or mass-wasting data;
- Wetlands Report field data and wetland boundary assumptions, especially Federal Parcel 2
  delineation results and refined preliminary wetland estimates for the remaining parcels;
- Roads/Trails/Access Report route status, trail classifications, easement tables, and INFRA route
  information;
- any prior soil, hydrology, road, wetland, range, vegetation, botany, minerals, hazardous-materials,
  or realty notes that identify soil-related issues.

### Fieldwork Plan

Submit a fieldwork plan for Forest Service review before mobilization. The plan must include:

- field questions and decision points;
- activity-area definitions for each soil-relevant action element;
- maps of proposed field review areas, soil map units, route alignments, wetland/riparian areas,
  stream crossings, slopes, and parcel boundaries;
- proposed observation points, transects, meander routes, representative soil checks, and photo
  points;
- method for applying Forest Service soil-disturbance classes to trail, trailhead, wetland/riparian,
  grazing-improvement, and parcel-management conditions;
- method for documenting why FW-STD-SOIL-01 does or does not apply to the linear trail features;
- access needs, landowner coordination needs, safety plan, communications plan, and seasonal
  constraints;
- field forms, GPS/photo naming conventions, GIS projection, and data-transfer format;
- coordination points with hydrology/wetlands, roads/trails/access, vegetation/botany, range,
  realty, minerals, hazardous materials, and Forest Plan consistency reviewers.

### Field Timing

Fieldwork must occur during a snow-free period when soil surface conditions can be observed. Avoid
field conclusions when frozen ground, snow cover, wildfire closure, unsafe access, or saturated
conditions prevent reliable evaluation. A targeted wet-season or post-storm revisit must be recommended
if runoff, drainage, wetland connectivity, erosion, or sediment delivery cannot be evaluated during the
primary field visit.

### Field Methods

At minimum, fieldwork must include:

- meander review of the Sweet Trunk Trail alignment, Inspiration Divide Trail reroute, and Big Timber
  Canyon Trailhead improvement area where access allows;
- targeted review of stream crossings, wetland/riparian approaches, slope breaks, toe slopes,
  drainages, seeps/springs, hydric-soil indicators, and sediment-routing pathways;
- verification of representative NRCS soil map units, slope classes, landforms, surface texture,
  coarse fragments, drainage, hydric or wet soil indicators, and erosion susceptibility;
- visual soil-disturbance classification for existing compaction, rutting, displacement, puddling,
  surface erosion, loss of surface organic matter, and mass-movement features;
- GPS/photo documentation of representative soil conditions, existing disturbance, sensitive
  features, stream/wetland crossings, drainage concerns, erosion features, and proposed disturbance
  footprints;
- shovel checks, soil probes, or soil pits where needed to verify hydric indicators, rooting depth,
  texture, restrictive layers, compaction, wetness, or restoration feasibility;
- review of wetland confirmation-test-pit assumptions from the Wetlands Report where soil/wetland
  boundaries affect trail, deed restriction, or sediment-delivery analysis;
- review of whether proposed trail design can meet hydrologic-disconnection, mass-wasting-avoidance,
  and hardened-crossing plan direction;
- review of soil conditions on federal parcels leaving ownership and non-federal parcels entering
  ownership where parcel access and project purpose require it.

Detailed lab sampling is not required unless the Forest Service soils reviewer determines a specific
question cannot be resolved by visual methods, soil survey, field checks, and professional judgment.

### Minimum Field Record

For each activity area or representative field check, record:

- date, observers, weather, soil moisture, and access limitations;
- activity-area ID and action element;
- GPS point, track, transect, or polygon identifier;
- photo IDs and captions;
- soil map unit and field verification notes;
- slope, landform, drainage position, hydric/wet soil indicators, and sediment-connectivity notes;
- existing disturbance type and soil-disturbance class;
- expected disturbance source and approximate footprint, length, or acreage;
- erosion, compaction, rutting, displacement, mass movement, organic matter, and ground-cover
  observations;
- applicable BMP, design feature, mitigation, or monitoring need;
- uncertainty, data gap, or revisit need.

## Analysis

Analysis must translate the field record and source documents into report-ready soil findings. It must
use the South Plateau soils report as the local structure model while staying tailored to East Crazies.

### Analysis Framework

The Soils Specialist Report must include:

- affected environment and existing condition;
- applicable Forest Plan standards and guidelines;
- methodology, including spatial and temporal scale;
- resource indicators and indicator measures;
- direct and indirect effects;
- cumulative effects;
- mitigation, BMPs, and monitoring;
- Forest Plan consistency;
- references and administrative-record index.

### Spatial And Temporal Scale

Define and justify spatial and temporal bounds.

Spatial bounds:

- Direct effects: Sweet Trunk Trail, Inspiration Divide Trail reroute, Big Timber Canyon Trailhead,
  access/easement work, stream/wetland approaches, restoration or revegetation sites, and any
  construction staging areas.
- Indirect effects: connected slopes, drainage paths, riparian management zones, wetlands, stream
  crossings, road/trail drainage networks, and sediment-routing areas.
- Cumulative effects: project boundary plus any watershed, route, allotment, parcel, or wetland
  context needed to evaluate additive soil disturbance and sediment effects from past, present, and
  reasonably foreseeable actions.

Temporal bounds:

- Existing condition: current field-observed and GIS/record condition.
- Short term: construction through stabilization/revegetation of disturbed soil.
- Long term: period during which trail use, trailhead use, parcel management, erosion, compaction,
  drainage, or revegetation could affect soil function.

### Resource Indicators

Use these indicators unless the Forest Service soils reviewer approves changes:

- DSD or equivalent soil-disturbance indicator for compaction, rutting, displacement, puddling,
  surface erosion, loss of surface organic matter, severe burning if relevant, and mass movement.
- Soil productivity and long-term site productivity.
- Hydrologic soil function, infiltration, drainage, and sediment delivery.
- Organic substrates, litter/duff, soil organic matter, and coarse woody debris where vegetation or
  forested activity areas are involved.
- Wet/hydric soil and riparian soil condition where trail, wetland, or stream-adjacent actions occur.
- Revegetation and invasive-species establishment risk.

The report must explicitly state whether FW-STD-SOIL-01 is applied, modified for linear trail
features, or screened out. If screened out, the report must still evaluate soil quality using the
indicators above and must explain why the Plan Consistency Table's earlier rationale remains valid or
needs revision.

### Existing Condition Analysis

Describe:

- soil map units, landforms, slopes, parent materials, hydrologic soil groups, drainage, erosion
  hazard, compaction/rutting limitations, revegetation limitations, hydric-soil indicators, and
  sensitive soil attributes;
- existing roads, trails, trailheads, stream crossings, grazing improvements, disturbed areas,
  wetland/riparian areas, and route-drainage conditions;
- existing soil disturbance in activity areas and representative parcels;
- soil conditions on federal parcels leaving ownership and non-federal parcels entering ownership,
  where relevant to the soil report and land exchange decision;
- current soil-water connectivity and sediment-routing pathways;
- data gaps and limits of confidence.

### Direct And Indirect Effects Analysis

Analyze no action, proposed action, Alternative 1 if retained for analysis, and any other alternative
provided by the Forest Service.

The analysis must address:

- soil disturbance from the new Sweet Trunk Trail, Inspiration Divide Trail reroute, Big Timber
  Canyon Trailhead improvements, easements, stream crossings, staging, and restoration;
- whether trail construction near or across waterbodies could deliver sediment, and whether magnitude
  and duration remain low with design features and LMP protections;
- whether the Inspiration Divide Trail reroute reduces soil/sediment effects by eliminating the
  existing unnecessary stream and wetland crossing;
- whether wetland deed restrictions and retained wetland parcels adequately protect wet/hydric soils,
  riparian soils, and soil-water functions;
- whether no-subdivision/no-mineral-development restrictions and wetland protective covenants reduce
  foreseeable soil disturbance on federal parcels leaving ownership;
- whether acquired parcels create new soil-management needs for NFS management;
- whether trailhead resurfacing, additional parking, toilet facilities, or kiosk construction need
  erosion-control, compaction, drainage, topsoil, or revegetation design criteria;
- whether grazing-related improvements or continued grazing affect compaction, bare soil, bank
  disturbance, erosion, or revegetation needs;
- whether hazardous-materials or mineral conditions create soil handling, avoidance, disclosure, or
  mitigation needs;
- whether restoration, invasive-species prevention, carbon, or vegetation assumptions rely on soil
  conditions.

Quantify expected disturbance by action element, activity area, soil map unit, and alternative when
data allow. For linear features where acreage-based DSD is not useful, disclose length, width,
crossing count, sensitive-soil crossings, wetland/riparian approaches, drainage features, and
representative soil-disturbance class instead.

### Cumulative Effects Analysis

Evaluate cumulative soil effects only where there is a useful cause-and-effect relationship. Screen:

- existing and proposed routes, trails, trailheads, unauthorized routes, and route drainage;
- grazing use and range improvements;
- wetland/riparian protections or disturbance;
- foreseeable trail construction, road maintenance, restoration, or recreation improvements;
- private land development risk on parcels leaving federal ownership, considering deed restrictions
  and protective covenants;
- vegetation, invasive species, fire/fuels, carbon, and restoration actions that could alter ground
  cover, erosion, or revegetation.

### Mitigation, BMPs, And Monitoring Analysis

Prepare a mitigation/BMP/monitoring matrix. Each row must include action element, soil concern,
location trigger, required measure, responsible party, timing, and verification method.

At minimum, analyze:

- hydrologic disconnection of trail drainage from riparian management zones and waterbodies;
- drainage spacing, rolling dips, grade reversals, waterbars, cross drains, armoring, outsloping, and
  sediment filtering;
- stream-crossing hardening for trail approaches, streambeds, banks, and approaches;
- avoidance or design modification for high mass-wasting potential, wet soils, hydric soils, steep
  slopes, unstable areas, and sensitive soil map units;
- construction timing and wet-weather restrictions;
- erosion and sediment control during trail, trailhead, and access work;
- topsoil/duff salvage, decompaction, revegetation, mulch, weed-free seed, weed prevention, and
  restoration;
- photo-point or post-construction monitoring for trail, trailhead, wetland/riparian, and
  revegetation areas;
- whether any DSD or soil-disturbance monitoring is needed even if FW-STD-SOIL-01 is not applied to
  trail building.

### Forest Plan Consistency Analysis

Evaluate and document consistency with:

- FW-DC-SOIL-01, FW-DC-SOIL-02, FW-DC-SOIL-03;
- FW-STD-SOIL-01, including the rationale for applicability or non-applicability;
- FW-GDL-SOIL-01 through FW-GDL-SOIL-08;
- road/trail guidelines applied in the Plan Consistency Table, especially FW-GDL-RT-03,
  FW-GDL-RT-06, and FW-GDL-RT-07;
- invasive-species and revegetation direction, including Forest Service standard operating
  procedures and BMPs for equipment/material cleaning and prompt revegetation after
  ground-disturbing management activities;
- riparian, wetland, aquatic, recreation, recommended-wilderness, backcountry, and designated-area
  components where they affect soil or sediment analysis.

The final report must explain where it agrees with, narrows, or revises the existing Plan Consistency
Table.

## Deliverables

Deliverables must be organized so the Forest Service can place them directly in the administrative
record and use them in EA text, Forest Plan consistency review, and implementation.

### Deliverable 1: Kickoff And Data Request Log

Provide:

- kickoff meeting notes;
- information received and missing;
- source documents reviewed;
- access constraints;
- stop conditions;
- Forest Service decisions needed before fieldwork.

Acceptance criteria:

- identifies missing GIS, parcel, trail design, wetland, route, or Forest Plan data;
- identifies whether the soil report will proceed as standalone report or no-issue memo pending
  field verification;
- identifies field-season and access constraints.

### Deliverable 2: Fieldwork Plan

Provide:

- field objectives;
- activity-area definitions;
- field maps;
- proposed routes, transects, observation points, soil checks, wetland/riparian checks, and photo
  points;
- field forms;
- safety/access plan;
- data naming conventions;
- schedule;
- coordination needs.

Acceptance criteria:

- covers Sweet Trunk Trail, Inspiration Divide reroute, Big Timber Canyon Trailhead, stream/wetland
  interfaces, and parcel restrictions;
- identifies how soil disturbance will be classified for linear trail features;
- identifies whether additional wet-season or post-storm verification may be needed.

### Deliverable 3: Field Data Package

Provide:

- GPS points, tracks, polygons, and activity-area refinements;
- completed field forms;
- geotagged photo log;
- soil check notes and soil map unit verification;
- wetland/riparian and sediment-connectivity notes;
- field findings memo identifying urgent design, BMP, mitigation, or monitoring issues.

Acceptance criteria:

- every material field conclusion is traceable to location, date, observer, photo, and activity area;
- sensitive soil, hydric/wet soil, erosion, compaction, rutting, displacement, drainage, and
  sediment-delivery concerns are mapped or explicitly screened out;
- data are suitable for Custer Gallatin GIS/ID-team use.

### Deliverable 4: Analysis Tables And Map Atlas

Provide:

- soil map unit and activity-area crosswalk;
- existing disturbance and expected disturbance table;
- action-element by soil concern matrix;
- stream/wetland crossing and sediment-connectivity table;
- mitigation/BMP/monitoring matrix;
- Forest Plan consistency matrix;
- data-gap and uncertainty table;
- project location, soil-analysis area, soil map unit, slope/erosion hazard, wetland/riparian,
  road/trail/trailhead, field observation, and mitigation maps.

Acceptance criteria:

- maps include scale, north arrow, coordinate system, data date, project boundary, activity-area IDs,
  and source attribution;
- tables distinguish no action, proposed action, and Alternative 1 or other alternatives;
- linear-feature effects are disclosed with useful measures, not forced into an acreage metric where
  that would mislead.

### Deliverable 5: Draft Soils Specialist Report

Provide a complete draft report with:

- executive summary;
- project description and alternatives;
- fieldwork and methods;
- affected environment;
- direct and indirect effects;
- cumulative effects;
- mitigation, BMPs, and monitoring;
- Forest Plan consistency;
- unavoidable adverse effects and productivity considerations if relevant;
- references and administrative-record index;
- appendices for field forms, photos, GIS layers, DSD/soil-disturbance calculations, and maps.

Acceptance criteria:

- follows the level of detail expected from the South Plateau soils specialist report while remaining
  tailored to East Crazies;
- explains whether a standalone soils report is required or whether a no-issue/incorporated-analysis
  memo is sufficient;
- explicitly addresses what East Crazies already did in the Plan Consistency Table, Roads/Trails
  Report, Wetlands Report, and final EA;
- ties every soil conclusion to field evidence, GIS evidence, source documents, or specialist
  judgment.

### Deliverable 6: Draft EA Soils Section

Provide concise EA-ready text for:

- affected environment;
- environmental consequences;
- mitigation/design criteria;
- Forest Plan consistency;
- cumulative effects if needed.

Acceptance criteria:

- suitable for insertion into an EA or appendix;
- references the soils specialist report rather than duplicating all technical detail;
- uses plain language for public disclosure.

### Deliverable 7: Final Soils Specialist Report

Provide:

- final report;
- final PDF;
- editable source file;
- comment-response or change log;
- final tables, maps, appendices, and administrative-record index.

Acceptance criteria:

- resolves Forest Service and ID-team comments;
- preserves source citations and field-data traceability;
- clearly states residual uncertainty and Forest Service decisions;
- final PDF starts with a valid `%PDF-` header.

### Deliverable 8: Final Data Package

Provide:

- GIS layers or geopackage/file geodatabase;
- PDF map atlas;
- photo log and photos;
- field forms;
- source data inventory;
- metadata/readme;
- data limitations memo.

Acceptance criteria:

- GIS and table outputs use agreed naming, projection, and metadata;
- the Forest Service can reproduce report maps and trace field conclusions;
- source-library or administrative-record file paths are documented.

Review gates:

- Forest Service approves fieldwork plan before mobilization.
- Forest Service reviews field findings before draft report production.
- Forest Service reviews draft report before draft EA text is finalized.
- Forest Service accepts final report before the decision record relies on soil mitigation, design
  criteria, or monitoring commitments.

## Open Forest Service Decisions

The Forest Service must resolve:

- whether `soil_resources` should be added as an explicit East Crazies proposed-action resource
  area;
- whether a standalone soils specialist report is required or whether an incorporated/no-issue memo
  is sufficient;
- whether FW-STD-SOIL-01 is not applicable to the trail work, and what alternative soil-quality
  indicator language should be used for the report;
- whether any field review is needed on both federal disposal and non-federal acquisition parcels;
- which action alternatives must be analyzed beyond no action, proposed action, and Alternative 1;
- which BMPs, mitigation, design criteria, and monitoring commitments must be carried into the EA,
  decision record, collection agreement, trail design package, or implementation file;
- which GIS/data package format is required by the Custer Gallatin ID team.
